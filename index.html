ERA 01
Proto-Social
1978 – 1996
Overview
The first online communities emerged long before the public internet — in dial-up bulletin boards, university text networks, and government research systems. These fragile, text-only hubs were the seeds of everything that followed.
Defining characteristics
Communities formed around shared hardware and phone lines. Access required technical knowledge. Identity was often anonymous or pseudonymous. Real-time communication existed but was rare and expensive. The social dynamics of today — flame wars, memes, spam, reputation — all appeared here first in primitive form.

ERA 02
Profile Web
1997 – 2006
Overview
The first platforms built around personal identity and friend graphs. Social networking moved from anonymous forums to real names, profile photos, and public relationship maps.
Defining characteristics
Platforms competed to become the definitive directory of human relationships. Real names began replacing handles. The "friend request" and "profile page" became social primitives. MySpace's HTML customization gave users their first true creative canvas online — laying the groundwork for personal branding.

ERA 03
Feed Era
2005 – 2012
Overview
Social media went mainstream. Platforms discovered the feed — an infinite, updating stream of content from your network — and it changed everything about how people consumed information online.
Defining characteristics
The news feed algorithmically collapsed individual profiles into a shared stream. Broadcasting replaced directory-browsing. Likes, retweets, and shares created new social currencies. YouTube democratized video publishing. The viral loop was invented. By 2012, social media had become infrastructure.

Napster
1999 · Peer-to-Peer
Shawn Fanning built Napster in his dorm room at 19. By 2000, over 20 million users shared 14,000 songs every minute. It terrified the music industry, spawned landmark copyright cases, and proved that peer-to-peer social sharing could upend entire industries.

ERA 04
Mobile & Visual
2010 – 2019
Overview
The smartphone relocated social media from the desk to the pocket and transformed it from text-based to image-first. The camera became the most important feature of any phone.
Defining characteristics
Instagram's filters turned ordinary moments into aesthetics. Snapchat's stories introduced ephemerality as a design principle. Vine proved that 6 seconds was enough for a cultural moment. Mobile-first design, AR filters, live streaming, and disappearing content all defined a generation's relationship with social visibility and performance.

ERA 05
Algorithmic Social
2020 – Present
Overview
The social graph gave way to the interest graph. You no longer needed to follow anyone — algorithms deliver content personalized to your behavior, whether you asked for it or not.
Defining characteristics
TikTok's For You Page was a paradigm shift: social recommendation without social connection. Short-form video became the dominant format across every platform. The creator economy professionalized content creation. Platforms became both entertainment networks and information ecosystems — raising new questions about attention, truth, and what "social" even means anymore.
